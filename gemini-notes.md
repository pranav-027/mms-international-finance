## 1. Introduction to International Finance

- **Definition:** International Finance is the study of monetary interactions between two or more countries[cite: 2]. It is a critical component of international economics that drives globalization, global trade, and foreign investments[cite: 2].
- **Scope and Key Areas:** The field covers the Balance of Payments, exchange rates, foreign exchange markets, global capital markets, international institutions (like the IMF and World Bank), and the management of currency, political, and interest rate risks[cite: 2].
- **Multinational Corporations (MNCs):** MNCs are companies that produce and sell goods or services in multiple countries, typically consisting of a home-country parent company and foreign subsidiaries[cite: 2]. They operate with a global perspective regarding where to build plants, sell products, raise capital, and hire personnel[cite: 2].
- **Real-World Application:** Cross-border mergers, acquisitions, and investments are prime examples of international finance[cite: 2]. For instance, Narayana Hrudayalaya's acquisition of the UK-based Practice Plus Group Hospitals in 2025 involved foreign direct investment (FDI), foreign exchange exposure, compliance with multiple regulatory environments, and international capital markets[cite: 2].

## 2. Balance of Payments (BOP)

### A. Core Concepts

- **Definition:** The Balance of Payments is a systematic statistical statement that records all economic transactions between the residents of a country and the rest of the world over a specific time period, typically a quarter or a year[cite: 1].
- **Accounting Principles:** It follows a double-entry principle where every inflow has a corresponding outflow, ensuring that the BOP always balances fundamentally[cite: 1]. For example, the export of goods causes commodities to flow out and foreign currency to flow in, matching the outflow of goods with an inflow of funds[cite: 1].
- **Standardization:** To facilitate accurate international comparisons, countries generally prepare their BOP in US Dollars, the world's reserve currency[cite: 1]. In India, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is the authority responsible for releasing BOP data based on Government of India statistics[cite: 1].

### B. Structure of the BOP

The BOP is divided into three primary accounts:

**1. Current Account**

- **Trade or Visible Account:** Records the physical movement, or export and import, of goods[cite: 1].
- **Service or Invisible Account:** Records intangible transactions, including service exports/imports, IT services, and remittances[cite: 1].
- **Primary Income (Income Transfers):** Cross-border payments related to factor incomes, such as interest, dividends, and the compensation of employees[cite: 1]. These are essentially payments for the use of factors of production like labor and capital[cite: 1].
- **Secondary Income (Current Transfers):** One-way transactions provided without a quid pro quo, such as worker remittances, gifts, and foreign aid[cite: 1]. For example, money sent home by a permanent migrant worker is a current transfer[cite: 1].

**2. Capital Account**

- This account handles capital transfers and transactions in non-produced, non-financial assets between residents and non-residents[cite: 1].
- Key components include the sale or purchase of land and real estate abroad, natural resource rights (like mining concessions), intangible assets (patents, copyrights, trademarks), and specific long-term leases[cite: 1].

**3. Financial Account**

- This account reflects net changes in the ownership of national assets and records the flow of capital in the form of investments[cite: 1].
- It includes Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI), other investments, and changes to official reserve assets[cite: 1].

### C. Regulatory and Institutional Oversight

- **The Bretton Woods Twins:** The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank Group were established in July 1944 at the Bretton Woods conference to help reconstruct economies after World War II[cite: 1].
- **IMF Role:** The IMF monitors global BOP transactions to prevent international trust deficits and ensure countries possess sufficient forex reserves for their international commitments[cite: 1].
- **Fiscal Discipline:** The IMF advises countries to keep their Current Account Deficit below 3% of their Gross Domestic Product (GDP)[cite: 1]. The deficit is calculated as: (Current Account Deficit / GDP) x 100[cite: 1].

## 3. Foreign Exchange (Forex) Market

### A. Market Mechanics

- **Definition:** The foreign exchange market is a global, decentralized over-the-counter (OTC) marketplace where currencies are traded and exchange rates are determined[cite: 2]. It involves the conversion of one country's currency into another, primarily facilitated by banks holding foreign currency balances[cite: 2].
- **Key Features:**
  - **Decentralization:** There is no single central exchange; it operates electronically worldwide[cite: 2].
  - **Continuous Operation:** The market functions 24 hours a day, five days a week[cite: 2].
  - **High Liquidity:** Trillions of dollars are traded daily, allowing participants to enter and exit positions without major price disruptions[cite: 2].
  - **Efficiency:** The market is characterized by high transparency and lower trading costs[cite: 2]. The US Dollar is the most widely traded currency[cite: 2].
- **Market Participants:** The ecosystem includes central banks, commercial banks, investment banks, governments, corporations, hedge funds, and retail traders[cite: 2].

### B. Determinants of Exchange Rates

Exchange rates in the forex market are influenced by several core factors:

- Interest rates set by Central Banks[cite: 2].
- Inflation rates and overall economic stability[cite: 2].
- Supply and demand dynamics for foreign currency[cite: 2].
- Market speculation and broader geopolitical events[cite: 2].

### C. Real-World Currency Fluctuation (Case Study)

- In early December 2025, the Indian Rupee (INR) breached the 90 per US Dollar mark for the first time, reaching record lows of 90.13 to 90.21[cite: 2].
- **Causes:** This depreciation was driven by sustained Foreign Portfolio Investor (FPI) outflows, a strengthening US dollar globally, rising crude oil prices, and uncertainty surrounding India-US trade talks[cite: 2].
- **Impact & Response:** The drop led to increased import costs and heightened inflation concerns[cite: 2]. While the RBI intervened to manage extreme volatility, it allowed the currency flexibility rather than strictly defending a fixed price level[cite: 2].
