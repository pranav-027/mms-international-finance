## MODULE 1: INTRODUCTION TO INTERNATIONAL FINANCE

### 1.1 Definition

International Finance is the study of monetary interactions between two or more countries. It is a branch of international economics that examines how money, capital, and financial instruments flow across national borders.

### 1.2 Scope and Key Areas

International Finance covers the following key areas:

- **Balance of Payments and Exchange Rates** - Tracking cross-border transactions and currency valuation
- **Foreign Exchange Markets** - How currencies are bought, sold, and priced globally
- **International Institutions** - Role of IMF, World Bank, and other global bodies
- **Risks** - Currency risk, political risk, and interest rate risk
- **Global Capital Markets** - Cross-border investment and financing

### 1.3 Importance

International Finance drives globalization, facilitates international trade, and enables cross-border investment. In an interdependent world economy, aspects such as payment imbalances and foreign investment play leading roles in economic and policy decisions.

### 1.4 Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

An MNC is a company engaged in producing and selling goods or services in more than one country. It typically consists of a parent company in the home country and several foreign subsidiaries.

Key characteristics:

- Characterized more by attitude than physical reality
- Takes a global perspective - asks "Where in the world should we build plants, sell products, raise capital, and hire personnel?"
- Operates an integrated system of marketing and production activities worldwide

### 1.5 Real-World Example: Narayana Hrudayalaya UK Acquisition

Narayana Hrudayalaya (Narayana Health) acquired UK-based Practice Plus Group Hospitals on October 31, 2025, valued at approximately GBP 189 million (~Rs. 1,930-2,200 crore). This is a textbook case of International Finance in action:

- **Cross-border investment (FDI)** - Capital flowing from India to the UK
- **Foreign exchange exposure** - Payments required in GBP creating currency risk
- **Regulatory environment** - Compliance with both Indian and UK financial regulations
- **Financing structure** - Use of international capital markets (loans, bonds, equity)
- **Strategic impact** - Expanded global presence, diversified revenue, exposure to international risks

## MODULE 2: BALANCE OF PAYMENTS (BOP)

### 2.1 Definition

**IMF's Balance of Payments Manual, 5th Edition (BPM5):**

"Balance of Payments is a statistical statement that systematically summarizes, for a specific time period, economic transactions of an economy with the rest of the world."

In simpler terms: the difference between all the money entering and exiting a country over a defined period such as a quarter or a year.

### 2.2 Purpose and Monitoring

- BOP helps monitor all international monetary transactions for a specific time period
- In India, BOP is prepared from April 1 to March 31 (financial year basis) - quarterly, monthly, and yearly
- Since the US Dollar is the world reserve currency, all countries prepare BOP in USD for easy cross-country comparison
- **In India, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI)** is the authority that releases BOP figures periodically after receiving data from the Government of India
- BOP of countries is monitored by the **IMF**

### 2.3 The Double-Entry Principle

Every transaction in the BOP has two sides - every inflow has a corresponding outflow. This is why BOP always balances.

**Example - Exports:**

- Goods flow OUT of the country (debit to merchandise)
- Foreign currency flows IN (credit to financial account)

**Example - Imports (Rs. 100 million worth of goods):**

- Physical goods come INTO the country
- Recorded as a DEBIT in the Current Account (outflow of foreign exchange)
- India sends foreign currency (USD, EUR) abroad
- Recorded as a reduction in reserves or increase in foreign liabilities

### 2.4 Structure of BOP - Three Main Accounts

#### A. Current Account

Records transactions related to goods, services, income, and transfers.

Sub-components:

1. **Trade Account (Visible Account)**
   - Export and import of physical goods
   - Visible because goods can be seen moving across borders
   - Exports = credit (+); Imports = debit (-)

2. **Services Account (Invisible Account)**
   - Service exports and imports (IT services, tourism, shipping)
   - Inward and Outward Remittances
   - Invisible because services cannot be seen

3. **Primary Income (Income Transfers)**
   - Cross-border payments related to factor incomes
   - Investment income: interest, dividends, profits
   - Compensation of employees (wages paid to workers abroad)
   - Factor incomes arise from the four factors of production:
     - Land → Rent
     - Labor → Wages/Salary
     - Capital → Interest/Dividends
     - Entrepreneurship → Profit
   - When a temporary worker abroad sends earnings home, it is an income transfer (factor income)

4. **Secondary Income (Current Transfers)**
   - One-way transactions with no quid pro quo
   - Remittances from permanent migrants abroad
   - Foreign aid for consumption
   - Gifts
   - When a permanent migrant abroad sends money home, it is a current transfer (remittance)

**Key distinction:**

- Temporary worker abroad → Income transfer (factor income)
- Permanent migrant abroad → Current transfer (remittance)

#### B. Capital Account

Records transactions involving the acquisition or disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets and capital transfers.

Includes:

- Capital transfers between residents and non-residents
- Non-financial asset transfers

**Non-Financial Asset Transfers - Examples:**

| Category                    | Examples                                                     | Notes                                                           |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Land and Real Estate Abroad | Sale/purchase of land or buildings in another country        | Recorded when ownership changes across borders                  |
| Natural Resource Rights     | Rights to exploit forests, mineral deposits, fishing grounds | Includes concessions or licenses granted to foreign entities    |
| Intangible Assets           | Patents, copyrights, trademarks, franchises                  | Covers intellectual property rights transferred internationally |
| Contracts and Leases        | Long-term leases or contracts granting usage rights          | Must involve cross-border ownership or usage rights             |
| Other Non-Produced Assets   | Spectrum rights, water rights, airspace rights               | Any asset not produced but with economic value                  |

#### C. Financial Account

Records transactions involving ownership of financial assets and liabilities between residents and non-residents.

Includes:

- **Direct Investment (FDI)** - Long-term investment for control
- **Portfolio Investment (FPI)** - Investment in stocks, bonds without control
- **Reserve Assets** - Changes in official foreign exchange reserves
- **Other Investments** - Loans, trade credits, currency deposits

### 2.6 BOP Always Balances

- If total inflows > total outflows, the excess goes to Forex Reserves (accretion)
- If total outflows > total inflows, the deficit is met from Forex Reserves (depletion)
- This is why BOP, as an accounting statement, always balances

### 2.7 Current Account Deficit (CAD) and IMF Guidelines

The IMF monitors BOP and advises countries to keep their **Current Account Deficit (CAD) below 3% of GDP** as fiscal discipline.

**Formula:**

> CAD as % of GDP = (Current Account Deficit / GDP) x 100

**IMF Evidence:**

- IMF External Sector Reports flag CAD above 3-5% of GDP as a warning zone
- For developing economies with weaker reserves, 3% of GDP is treated as a safe upper bound
- Global current account imbalances widened to 3.6% of world GDP in 2024

**Example Calculation:**

- Assume: Balance of Trade deficit + Balance of Invisibles deficit = $100 billion
- Assume: GDP of country = $3,000 billion
- CAD% = (100/3000) x 100 = **3.33%** → exceeds the 3% threshold → requires policy attention

## MODULE 3: IMF AND WORLD BANK - THE BRETTON WOODS TWINS

### 3.1 Background

The IMF and World Bank Group were set up in **July 1944** at a conference held in **Bretton Woods, USA**. They were established to assist in the reconstruction of war-ravaged countries after World War II (1940-1944). The key architects were:

- **John Maynard Keynes** (United Kingdom)
- **Harry Dexter White** (United States)

Because both institutions were born at Bretton Woods, they are collectively called the **Bretton Woods Twins**.

### 3.2 Role of IMF in BOP

- Monitors BOP transactions of member countries
- Advises countries on fiscal discipline (CAD < 3% of GDP)
- Provides financial assistance to countries facing BOP crises
- Publishes External Sector Reports (ESR) assessing global external balances
- Publishes Finance and Development journal tracking global imbalances

### 3.3 Forex Reserves

- Cross-border transactions are mostly settled in US Dollars
- Every country's Central Bank must possess sufficient forex reserves to meet international obligations
- If settlements are not met, it leads to trust deficit and losses for creditors
- In India, RBI maintains and manages forex reserves

## MODULE 4: FOREIGN EXCHANGE MARKET (FOREX MARKET)

### 4.1 Definition

Foreign Exchange (Forex/FX) is the mechanism by which the currency of one country is converted into the currency of another country.

The **Foreign Exchange Market** is a global decentralized or over-the-counter (OTC) market for the trading of currencies. It determines foreign exchange rates for every currency and includes all aspects of buying, selling, and exchanging currencies at current or determined prices.

### 4.2 Key Features of the Forex Market

1. **Decentralization** - Unlike stock exchanges, the forex market operates globally and electronically, with trading occurring 24 hours a day, 5 days a week. It has no central exchange or physical location.

2. **High Liquidity** - The forex market is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with trillions of dollars traded daily. Participants can easily enter and exit positions without significant price movements.

3. **Diverse Participants** - Central banks, commercial banks, hedge funds, multinational corporations, retail traders, and speculators. Each has different objectives - from hedging currency risk to seeking profit from currency fluctuations.

4. **Market Transparency** - Prices are generally visible to all participants

5. **Dynamic Market** - Continuously evolving based on global economic events

6. **Lower Trading Cost** - Transaction costs are relatively low

7. **Dollar Most Widely Traded** - USD is the dominant currency in global forex trading

### 4.3 Participants in the Forex Market

- Retail traders
- Commercial banks
- Central banks
- Corporations
- Governments
- Investment banks
- Hedge funds

### 4.4 How Exchange Rates Are Determined

Foreign exchange rates are determined by:

1. **Interest Rates set by Central Banks** - Higher interest rates attract foreign capital, strengthening the currency
2. **Inflation and Economic Stability** - Lower inflation leads to currency appreciation
3. **Market Speculation and Geopolitical Events** - Expectations and political risk affect currency values
4. **Supply and Demand for Foreign Currency Trading** - Basic market forces

### 4.5 Currency Conversion Mechanism

The conversion of currency is done by banks that deal in foreign exchange. These banks maintain stocks of foreign currencies in the form of balances with correspondent banks abroad.

### 4.6 Case Study - Indian Rupee Crossing Rs. 90 per USD (December 2025)

The Indian Rupee (INR) crossed the Rs. 90 per US Dollar mark for the first time in early December 2025 (around December 3, 2025), hitting record lows of Rs. 90.13-90.21.

**Causes:**

- Investor Outflows: Significant selling of Indian equities by Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs)
- Trade Uncertainty: Stalled India-US trade talks
- Strong Dollar: Strengthening US Dollar against global currencies
- High Crude Oil Prices: Rising oil prices weighing on the currency

**Impact:**

- Increased import costs (especially oil)
- Higher inflation
- Challenging environment for importers and families

**RBI Action:**

- RBI intervened to manage extreme volatility
- Analysts suggested allowing more exchange rate flexibility rather than defending a fixed level

## MODULE 5: RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN BOP AND FOREX

The BOP and the forex market are deeply interconnected:

- A Current Account Deficit means more foreign exchange is flowing OUT than coming IN → puts downward pressure on the domestic currency
- When a country's currency depreciates, exports become cheaper (more competitive) and imports become costlier → this tends to correct the Current Account Deficit over time (J-Curve effect)
- Capital Account surpluses (FDI, FPI inflows) can finance a Current Account Deficit
- The RBI uses forex reserves to stabilize the rupee when there is excessive volatility
