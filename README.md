## UNIT 1: INTRODUCTION TO INTERNATIONAL FINANCE

### 1.1 Definition and Scope

International Finance is the study of monetary interactions between two or more countries. It is a branch of international economics that examines how capital, currency, and financial instruments move across borders.

Key areas covered under International Finance:

- Balance of Payments (BOP) and Exchange Rates
- Foreign Exchange Markets
- International Institutions (IMF, World Bank)
- Currency, political, and interest rate risks
- Global Capital Markets

### 1.2 Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

An MNC is a company engaged in producing and selling goods or services in more than one country. It typically consists of a parent company in the home country and several foreign subsidiaries.

Key characteristics:

- Characterized more by attitude (global mindset) than by physical presence alone
- Operates with a global perspective: decisions on where to build plants, sell products, raise capital, and hire personnel are made from a worldwide standpoint
- Engages in cross-border investment, foreign exchange exposure, and multinational regulatory compliance

Practical Example - Narayana Hrudayalaya's UK Acquisition (2025):

- Narayana Health acquired UK-based Practice Plus Group Hospitals on October 31, 2025, valued at approximately GBP 189 million (around Rs. 1,930-2,200 crore)
- Executed through a step-down subsidiary: Narayana Hrudayalaya UK
- This deal illustrates International Finance in practice: it involved FDI, currency risk, global capital markets access, and multinational strategy
- Strategic purpose: entry into the UK healthcare market, focus on day-care procedures, and expanding Dr. Devi Shetty's vision of affordable private healthcare globally

## UNIT 2: BALANCE OF PAYMENTS (BOP)

### 2.1 Definition

As per the IMF's Balance of Payments Manual, 5th Edition (BPM5):

"Balance of Payments is a statistical statement that systematically summarizes, for a specific time period, economic transactions of an economy with the rest of the world."

In simpler terms: BOP is the difference between all money entering and exiting a country over a defined period (a quarter or a year).

### 2.2 Key Characteristics of BOP

- It is a systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world
- Transactions include trade in goods, services, income, transfers, and capital flows
- It is prepared using the double-entry principle: every inflow has a corresponding outflow, so BOP always balances
- Reported in US Dollars since USD is the world reserve currency, enabling easy cross-country comparison
- In India, prepared on financial year basis (April to March) and released by RBI quarterly and half-yearly
- BOP is monitored globally by the IMF

### 2.3 Double-Entry Principle Explained

For Exports:

- Goods flow out of the country, but foreign currency flows in
- The outflow of commodities is matched by the inflow of funds
- Net effect: balanced

For Imports:

- Physical goods come into the country (debit in Current Account)
- Foreign exchange is sent abroad to pay for goods
- Recorded as a reduction in reserves or an increase in foreign liabilities

### 2.4 Components of BOP

BOP has three major accounts:

---

#### A. Current Account

The Current Account records transactions related to income and consumption. It has four sub-components:

1. Trade/Visible Account
   - Export and import of physical/tangible goods
   - Called "visible" because the movement of goods can be physically seen
   - Difference between exports and imports = Balance of Trade (BOT)

2. Services/Invisible Account
   - Transactions in services which cannot be physically seen
   - Includes: IT exports/imports, travel, banking, insurance
   - Also includes inward and outward remittances

3. Income Transfers (Primary Income)
   - Cross-border payments related to factor incomes
   - Factors of production and their returns:
     - Land -> Rent
     - Labor -> Wages/Salary
     - Capital -> Interest/Dividends
     - Entrepreneurship -> Profit
   - When income flows across borders (e.g., wages paid to foreign workers or dividends sent to foreign shareholders), it is classified as a factor income transfer
   - Distinction: A temporary worker abroad earns factor income; that money sent home is an income transfer

4. Current Transfers (Secondary Income)
   - One-way transactions with no quid pro quo
   - Examples: foreign aid for consumption, gifts, grants
   - Remittances from permanent migrants are classified here
   - Distinction: A permanent migrant's income sent back home is a current transfer (remittance), not an income transfer

Key Distinction - Temporary vs Permanent Worker:

- Temporary worker abroad -> Income transfer (factor income)
- Permanent migrant abroad -> Current transfer (remittance)

---

#### B. Capital Account

Records transactions involving acquisition or disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets between residents and non-residents.

Includes two sub-components:

1. Capital Transfers
   - One-way transfers of ownership of fixed assets or debt forgiveness
   - Example: debt write-offs, migrants' transfers of assets

2. Non-Financial Asset Transfers
   - Transactions involving assets not produced through the production process but having economic value

Categories of non-financial assets:

| Category                    | Examples                                                     | Notes                                                           |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Land and Real Estate Abroad | Sale/purchase of land or buildings in another country        | Recorded when ownership changes across borders                  |
| Natural Resource Rights     | Rights to exploit forests, mineral deposits, fishing grounds | Includes concessions or licenses granted to foreign entities    |
| Intangible Assets           | Patents, copyrights, trademarks, franchises                  | Covers intellectual property rights transferred internationally |
| Contracts and Leases        | Long-term leases or contracts granting usage rights          | Must involve cross-border ownership or usage rights             |
| Other Non-Produced Assets   | Spectrum rights, water rights, airspace rights               | Any asset not produced but with economic value                  |

---

#### C. Financial Account

Records changes in ownership of financial assets and liabilities between residents and non-residents.

Includes:

1. Direct Investment (FDI - Foreign Direct Investment)
   - Long-term investment where the investor has significant control/influence
   - Example: A company setting up a factory abroad

2. Portfolio Investment (FPI - Foreign Portfolio Investment)
   - Investment in financial instruments (stocks, bonds) without management control
   - More liquid and volatile than FDI

3. Reserve Assets
   - Foreign exchange reserves held by the central bank (RBI in India)
   - Acts as a buffer: if total outflows exceed total inflows, the deficit is met from reserves
   - If inflows exceed outflows, surplus is added to reserves

### 2.6 Current Account Deficit (CAD) and IMF Guidelines

The IMF advises countries to keep their Current Account Deficit (CAD) below 3% of GDP as a measure of fiscal discipline.

Formula:

    CAD as % of GDP = (Current Account Deficit / GDP) x 100

Example Calculation:

- Current Account Deficit = $100 billion
- GDP = $3,000 billion
- CAD % = (100 / 3,000) x 100 = 3.33%
- This exceeds the IMF's advised threshold of 3%, signaling a need for corrective policy

IMF Evidence on CAD Thresholds:

- External Sector Reports flag deficits exceeding 3-5% of GDP as a warning zone
- Country reports from the 1980s-2000s flagged deficits above 5% of GDP as risky, especially if financed by short-term debt
- 3% of GDP is generally treated as a safe upper bound for developing economies

### 2.7 Role of IMF in BOP Monitoring

- The IMF was established in July 1944 at the Bretton Woods Conference (along with the World Bank Group), based on recommendations by economists John Maynard Keynes (UK) and Harry Dexter White (US)
- IMF and World Bank are collectively referred to as the Bretton Woods Twins
- IMF's role in BOP: monitors international transactions, ensures countries meet their external payment commitments, and provides financial assistance when needed
- Every country's central bank must hold sufficient forex reserves to meet international commitments
- Failure to meet settlements creates trust deficits and financial losses

### 2.8 India's BOP - Practical Notes

- RBI is the authority responsible for releasing India's BOP data
- Data is released quarterly; for example, the latest BOP data released by RBI on December 24, 2024 covered Q2 (July-September 2024) and the half-year April-September 2024
- India prepares its BOP in USD terms, in line with global practice

## UNIT 3: FOREIGN EXCHANGE (FOREX) MARKET

### 3.1 What is Foreign Exchange?

Foreign exchange is the mechanism by which the currency of one country is converted into the currency of another. This conversion is carried out by banks that deal in foreign exchange and maintain stocks of foreign currencies in the form of balances with correspondent banks abroad.

### 3.2 Definition of Forex Market

The Foreign Exchange Market (also known as Forex, FX, or currency market) is a global decentralized or over-the-counter (OTC) market for the trading of currencies.

- It determines foreign exchange rates for every currency
- Includes all aspects of buying, selling, and exchanging currencies at current or determined prices

### 3.3 Key Features of the Forex Market

1. Decentralization
   - Unlike stock exchanges with centralized physical locations, the forex market operates globally and electronically
   - Trades occur 24 hours a day, five days a week
   - No central exchange or physical location

2. High Liquidity
   - The forex market is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world
   - Trillions of dollars are traded daily
   - Participants can enter and exit positions without causing significant price movements

3. Diverse Participants
   - Central banks, commercial banks, investment banks, hedge funds, multinational corporations, retail traders, governments, and speculators
   - Each participant has different objectives: hedging currency risk, speculation, trade settlement, or reserve management

4. Other Features
   - Market transparency
   - Dynamic and continuously evolving
   - Lower trading costs compared to other financial markets
   - US Dollar is the most widely traded currency

### 3.4 How Exchange Rates are Determined

Exchange rates are influenced by the following factors:

1. Interest Rates Set by Central Banks
   - Higher interest rates attract foreign capital, strengthening the currency

2. Inflation and Economic Stability
   - Countries with lower inflation tend to have stronger currencies

3. Market Speculation and Geopolitical Events
   - Investor sentiment, political instability, and global events affect exchange rates

4. Supply and Demand for Foreign Currency
   - High demand for a currency (due to trade or investment) appreciates it; low demand depreciates it

### 3.5 Case Study - INR Crossing Rs. 90 per USD (December 2025)

Event: In early December 2025, the Indian Rupee crossed the psychologically significant Rs. 90 per USD mark for the first time, touching record lows around Rs. 90.13-90.21.

Causes:

- Investor Outflows: Significant FPI selling of Indian equities
- Trade Uncertainty: Stalled India-US trade talks
- Strong Dollar: US dollar strengthening globally
- High Crude Oil Prices: Adding pressure on India's import bill

Impact:

- Increased import costs, particularly for crude oil
- Higher inflation
- Challenging environment for importers
- Families affected through higher cost of goods

RBI Response:

- The Reserve Bank of India intervened in the forex market to manage extreme volatility
- Analysts suggested RBI adopted a less aggressive defense of fixed levels, allowing more currency flexibility

Managerial Implication: This event underscores the importance of exchange rate risk management for businesses with import-heavy operations or foreign currency borrowings.

## UNIT 4: KEY CONCEPTUAL LINKAGES FOR EXAM

### 4.1 Relationship between BOP and Exchange Rates

- A persistent Current Account Deficit (excess imports over exports) puts downward pressure on the domestic currency
- A strong Capital Account (FDI/FPI inflows) can offset CAD and support the currency
- The Rupee depreciation in 2025 was partly driven by FPI outflows, which negatively impacted India's Capital/Financial Account

### 4.3 Frequently Examined Distinctions

| Term A           | Term B              | Key Difference                                                                    |
| ---------------- | ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Current Account  | Capital Account     | Current: income/trade; Capital: ownership of assets                               |
| Balance of Trade | Balance of Payments | BOT: only goods; BOP: goods + services + capital + financial flows                |
| FDI              | FPI                 | FDI: control and long-term; FPI: no control, short-term and liquid                |
| Income Transfer  | Current Transfer    | Income: temporary worker wages; Current: permanent migrant remittances            |
| Visible Trade    | Invisible Trade     | Visible: physical goods; Invisible: services                                      |
| CAD              | Surplus             | CAD: outflows exceed inflows in current account; Surplus: inflows exceed outflows |
